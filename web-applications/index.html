Web applications

The Web made the power of computers available to people like nothing before. And then the mobile Web, on our phones and tablets, made it all available to us everywhere. Anyone with a smart phone can find pretty much any information at any time, via Google searches, Wikipedia, or many other sources.

Not only can we find information, we can learn, watch movies, play games, pay bills, do our banking, and any number of other tasks.

But most business are still struggling to reach that level of sharing, learning, and doing inside the business.

There are many good reasons for this, but basically, it's darn expensive and risky to develop software. Fortunately, a number of the technical reasons for the cost and risk have disappeared in the last five to ten years. Modern Web development frameworks, open source software, and cloud based platforms, among other things, have made it much easier, faster, cheaper, and therefore less risky, to develop Web applications. Applications that bring the power of the Web to the internal or external operations of medium and even small-sized businesses.

They also empower small teams to deliver quality, usable software. Small teams are more productive than big teams, because less time is wasted communicating between team members. And small teams are less risky than big teams, because the broken telephone effect is less of a problem.

Web apps are particularly suited to database applications. In these applications all users have access to the latest dataset and problems associated with data synchronization can be greatly simplified.
